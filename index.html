October

ORTEX Perspectives on the S&P 500 for October

October proved to be a volatile month, with the major indexes breaching the June lows to set fresh year-to-date lows before staging a strong rebound to finish the month higher. Short interest changes across the S&P 500 sectors were mixed. The biggest decline in short interest was within the energy sector, which saw a 13.8% decline in short interest shares to 390.8 million. Consumer discretionary stocks saw the largest increase in bearish sentiment, with short shares rising 2.8% to 875.8 million.

As the S&P 500 advanced throughout the back half of October, short sellers started to accumulate heavy losses. The bears ended up losing an aggregate $37.07 billion for the entire month.

With earnings season commencing, investors are seeing more meaningful changes in forward-looking EPS estimates within the S&P 500. The consumer discretionary sector saw earnings estimates get slashed by 8.7% overall, while real estate stocks saw a 0.9% increase.

The macroeconomic uncertainties that are impacting broader investor sentiment may also be weighing on insider activity, as there was virtually no meaningful buying activity among S&P 500 constituent insiders. For all levels of ORTEX Trade Significance ratings, there was just $3.1 million worth of purchases, compared to nearly $3.5 billion in insider stock sales. That results in a miniscule insider buying ratio of 0.9%

Sentiment among options traders appears to have improved, based on ORTEX Options Flow data for the SPY ETF. There were $14.55 billion in positive orders, compared to $5.47 billion worth of negative orders, translating into a positive order value ratio of 72%. That proportion is up from the 68% positive order value ratio for SPY in September.

Market developments in October

The month of October started off with a strong rally, only to see pessimism return. The major indexes then started to sell off ahead of the monthly Consumer Price Index (CPI) report, as investors continue to fret over persistent inflation. The CPI data showed that prices jumped 8.2% year-over-year in the month of September, representing a modest improvement from August’s 8.3% increase.

The report initially sparked aggressive selling, causing the indexes to breach the 2022 lows set in June before staging a stunning reversal throughout the balance of that day and closing higher. The market mostly maintained that upward momentum for the rest of October and finished with a monthly gain.

Earnings season also kicked off in October, giving investors the latest updates around corporate profitability. The quarterly results from major tech companies largely disappointed the market, pointing to a broader slowdown in the global advertising industry while cooling consumer demand for PCs led to weakness in that market.

For the month, the Dow Jones Industrial Average rallied 14%, the S&P 500 added 8%, and the Nasdaq Composite advanced by 3.9%.
